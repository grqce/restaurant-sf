What actually causes failures — 6 findings from 21K inspections

Critical · Inspector effect
The same restaurant would likely get different outcomes depending on who shows up
Inspector failure rates range from 0% to 15.4% — a 15x gap. In the Mission neighborhood alone, one inspector (Jessica Jang) failed 20% of restaurants while another (Sharon Aguila-Leonard) failed 0% across 50 inspections. This isn't fully explained by territory differences. It suggests a significant subjective component in what counts as a "failure." A logistic regression model trained on neighborhood + permit type + timing achieves only AUC 0.667, confirming that inspector identity is an unmodeled variable with major explanatory power.
Critical · Temporal effect
Monday inspections fail at nearly double the rate of Friday inspections
Monday failure rate: 7.2%. Friday failure rate: 3.7%. The gradient is monotonic — each day of the week is safer than the one before it. This is a classic "inspector fatigue in reverse" — inspectors may be more vigilant early in the week, or restaurants may be less prepared at the start of the week after weekend operations. Either way, your inspection day matters almost as much as your violations.
Notable · Seasonal effect
Summer and fall are meaningfully more dangerous than spring
Failure rates spike in August–September (6.3–6.4%) and dip to a low of 4.0% in April. This is likely a real food safety signal: warmer temperatures increase bacterial growth risk, and restaurants under-equipped for temperature control fail more. The seasonal pattern in average violations follows the same curve, suggesting this is about actual conditions, not just inspector behavior.
Notable · Geography
Japantown fails at 3.5x the rate of Potrero Hill — even controlling for restaurant type
Japantown: 13.2% failure rate. Oceanview/Merced/Ingleside: 10.5%. Potrero Hill: 0.2%. The neighborhood effect is large and persistent. High-violation neighborhoods (Mission avg 5.1 violations, Lakeshore 5.6) cluster differently from high-failure-rate neighborhoods, suggesting that violations alone don't explain geography — enforcement patterns and business demographics matter too.
Interesting · Permit type
Farmers markets close more than restaurants — but for different reasons
Certified Farmers Markets have a 6.5% closure rate vs 2.7% for small restaurants. But their average violation count is only 1.75 — the lowest of any category. This suggests closures are triggered by permit/licensing issues rather than food safety violations. Three farmers market operators have each been closed 3 times, pointing to a systemic compliance gap in how temporary vendors manage their permits.
Interesting · Repeat behavior
A small number of businesses account for a disproportionate share of closures
Two restaurants have been closed 5 times since Jan 2024 and are still operating. Five have been closed 4 times. The city's enforcement model appears to allow repeated closure-and-reopening rather than escalating to license revocation. This is either a policy gap or a data artifact worth investigating in person.
